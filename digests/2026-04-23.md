## Top Stories

### [Alberta startup sells no-tech tractors for half price](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47865868)
A Canadian startup is selling tractors with rebuilt 1990s Cummins diesel engines and zero modern electronics for half the price of a new John Deere. The move bypasses modern emissions regulations that mandate complex ECUs and DEF systems. HN commenters note this is driven as much by the desire for reliability and the right to repair as it is by cost, highlighting the growing market for "dumb" machinery.

### [Windows 9x Subsystem for Linux](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47861270)
A developer spent six years reverse-engineering Windows 9x internals to run a modern Linux kernel cooperatively inside it, without hardware virtualization. The project, proudly written without AI, essentially creates a "WSL" for Windows 95. The HN thread is full of awe at the sheer technical wizardry and perversity of the hack.

### [Qwen3.6-27B: Flagship-Level Coding in a 27B Dense Model](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47863217)
Alibaba released Qwen3.6-27B, a dense model that claims to match or beat Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Opus on coding benchmarks. The HN community is highly impressed with its local performance, noting that it can run comfortably on a 24GB GPU or a Mac with 32GB RAM. Commenters report it is highly capable for agentic coding workflows, significantly closing the gap between open-weights and frontier models.

### [We found a stable Firefox identifier linking all your private Tor identities](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47866697)
Fingerprint.com researchers disclosed a vulnerability in Firefox's IndexedDB implementation where the UUID ordering of databases creates a stable, high-entropy identifier that persists across origins and private browsing sessions. This allowed tracking Tor Browser users even after they requested a "New Identity." Tor Browser quickly released an update based on Mozilla's patch.

### [Apple fixes bug that cops used to extract deleted chat messages from iPhones](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47868867)
Apple patched a vulnerability in iOS 18 that allowed law enforcement to extract deleted Signal and WhatsApp messages. The issue stemmed from the OS caching the plaintext content of push notifications in a local database, bypassing the apps' end-to-end encryption. The thread highlights the ongoing privacy risk of OS-level notification services handling sensitive message content.
