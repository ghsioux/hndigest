## Top Stories

### [Previewing GPT-5.6 Sol: a next-generation model](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48689028)
OpenAI announced GPT-5.6 Sol, its latest frontier model, alongside a notable infrastructure detail: a Cerebras-hosted variant delivering up to 750 tokens per second, arriving in July with initial access limited to select customers. Technical discussion focused almost entirely on that speed number, with commenters arguing that 3x faster inference would close the gap between human developers and agentic coding harnesses more decisively than any benchmark improvement.

### [U.S. government will decide who gets to use GPT-5.6](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48690101)
Access to GPT-5.6 will require U.S. government vetting before any organization is cleared to deploy it, making this the first commercial frontier model to demand federal approval. The thread's top comment framed it as regulatory capture: entry barriers that protect incumbents while doing nothing to stop state actors or anyone capable of training their own weights. Others pushed back that open-weights models like DeepSeek already make U.S.-only gatekeeping cosmetic, and asked aloud whether GPU regulation is the logical next step.

### [U.S. allows Anthropic to release Mythos AI to 'trusted' US organizations](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48692995)
Anthropic received government clearance to distribute its Mythos model, but only to a vetted list of domestic organizations, forming a second data point for government-filtered frontier AI in a single news cycle. The discussion was blunt about what this means for Anthropic's financial future: DeepSeek V4 already handles most tasks at a fraction of the API cost, and a startup whose addressable market is government-screened looks, as one commenter put it, more like a missile factory than a consumer tech company.

### [We can still stop California's 3D printer surveillance scheme](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48692051)
The EFF is urging opposition to a California bill that would require 3D printers to log owner identities and record every print job, with law enforcement notification triggers built in. Commenters were broadly opposed on privacy grounds, though the thread's most memorable moment was a parent who received a call from his kindergartner's school principal: the offending print was a tiny Mandalorian figurine clutching a blaster pistol.
