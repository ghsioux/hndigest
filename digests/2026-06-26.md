## Top Stories

### [An entire Herculaneum scroll has been read for the first time](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48675179)
The Vesuvius Challenge team has completed a full virtual reading of PHerc 1667 (Scroll 4), a work by Aristocreon dated to roughly 200 BC — the first time an entire Herculaneum scroll has been read since the 79 AD eruption destroyed the villa's library. The result combines high-resolution CT scanning, ML-based virtual unwrapping, and ink-detection models refined over years of incremental progress; a preprint is available at scrollprize.org. The HN thread (259 comments) is full of wonder at a pipeline that links ancient papyrus, particle accelerators, and computer vision, with several contributors who worked on segmentation and ink detection sharing technical context.

### [Apple raises prices of MacBooks, iPads](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48672732)
Apple has sharply raised prices across MacBooks, iPads, and peripheral hardware, citing soaring memory costs — the 13-inch MacBook Air jumps $200 to $1,299, the M3 Ultra Mac Studio rises $1,300 to $5,299, and even the HomePod mini sees a $30 increase. The HN thread (1,001 comments) contextualises the move alongside simultaneous Xbox and PlayStation price hikes: commenters cite DRAM and NAND prices rising 2.5× since late 2025 and Micron separately locking in historically high memory prices for five years, suggesting this is an industry-wide supply-side shock rather than an Apple-specific margin move.

### [The 'papers, please' era of the internet will decimate your privacy](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48679608)
An EFF-linked article on expression.fire.org argues that online age-verification mandates being adopted across jurisdictions will normalise identity checks, create centralised honeypots of verified personal data, and fundamentally erode anonymous access to the web. The HN discussion (290 comments) explores anonymous-credential schemes — cryptographic techniques that can prove age thresholds without linking requests — as a technically viable alternative, with several commenters questioning whether governments have any genuine interest in privacy-preserving designs or whether surveillance is the actual goal.

### [IBM debuts sub-1 nanometer chip technology](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48674967)
IBM announced a "0.7 nm" (7 angstrom) chip technology built around a new nanostack architecture combining 3D stacking and novel materials. Commenters (164 comments) note that horizontal feature sizes remain in the 5 nm range and the node naming follows industry marketing conventions rather than physical dimensions, but acknowledge the engineering achievement as a genuine step toward angstrom-era scaling and a demonstration that continued transistor density improvements are still possible beyond conventional CMOS limits.
