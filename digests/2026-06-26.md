## Top Stories

### [An entire Herculaneum scroll has been read for the first time](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48675179)
The Vesuvius Challenge team has decoded a complete Herculaneum scroll charred by the 79 AD eruption, using ML-based ink detection on CT scans; the author is identified as Aristocreon, writing around 200 BC. Rather than debating the method, comments fixated on the uncanny reach: recovering words from a man who died two millennia ago from atoms preserved by a volcano, something no contemporary could have anticipated.

### [Apple raises prices of MacBooks, iPads](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48672732)
Apple has raised US prices across its Mac, iPad, Apple TV, and HomePod lineups by $100 to $1,300, citing higher memory costs; the M3 Ultra Mac Studio now starts at $5,299, up from $3,999. Whether tariffs are the real driver or cover for margin expansion dominated the argument, with many observers noting Microsoft's simultaneous Xbox price hike as circumstantial support for the former.

### [The 'papers, please' era of the internet will decimate your privacy](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48679608)
FIRE's essay argues that age-verification mandates spreading across state and national legislatures will transform the internet into an identity checkpoint, browsing history tied to government-issued credentials. The discussion split sharply: some engineers pointed to anonymous credential schemes such as blind signatures as a privacy-preserving middle ground, while skeptics countered that any system preventing token resale ultimately requires tying credentials to a traceable real-world identity, defeating the premise.

### [IBM debuts sub-1 nanometer chip technology](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48674967)
IBM announced a 0.7 nm "nanostack" chip architecture, claiming it pushes semiconductor scaling below the 1 nm node toward angstrom-scale dimensions. Skepticism was immediate: the actual features are far larger than the node label, and the industry's habit of decoupling brand names from physical dimensions long predates this announcement. The stacking technique was acknowledged as genuine progress, but the marketing-to-measurement gap drew the most pointed commentary.
